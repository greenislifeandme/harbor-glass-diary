Understanding Dormancy Periods and Why Winter Care Differs

When winter arrives, many plant owners keep doing what worked in summer—watering on the same schedule, fertilizing “to help,” and expecting steady growth. Then leaves yellow, stems soften, fungus gnats appear, or a once-thriving plant seems to stall. This isn’t necessarily a sign you’re doing something “wrong” so much as a sign your plant has entered a different operating mode.

Dormancy is best understood as a survival strategy: plants slow down when conditions are unfavorable, conserving energy and reducing demand for water and nutrients. Indoors, dormancy can be partial (growth slows but doesn’t fully stop). Outdoors, it can be dramatic (perennials die back, deciduous trees drop leaves). Either way, winter care differs because the environment differs—and plant physiology follows.

What Dormancy Really Is (and What It Isn’t)

Dormancy isn’t a single switch labeled “off.” It’s a spectrum of slowed metabolic activity driven by environmental signals such as day length, temperature, and light intensity. During dormancy or semi-dormancy:

  • Cell division slows, so new leaves and roots form more slowly.
  • Transpiration decreases, meaning the plant loses less water through its leaves.
  • Nutrient demand drops, because growth is the main “sink” that uses fertilizers efficiently.

Crucially, dormancy is not the same as sickness. A healthy plant in dormancy may look quieter—fewer new leaves, slower drying soil, less dramatic color. Problems begin when our care routine doesn’t match the new pace.

Different species also have different patterns. Many temperate plants have strong winter dormancy. Many tropical houseplants don’t go fully dormant, but they still slow down in typical indoor winter conditions because of reduced light and cooler nights near windows.

The Winter Environment: Why Your Home Becomes a Different Habitat

Even if your thermostat says 70°F, winter changes a plant’s world in subtle ways:

  • Light intensity drops: The sun sits lower in the sky and days are shorter, reducing the “daily light integral” plants receive. Windows also filter light, and cloudy weather compounds the deficit.
  • Temperature swings increase: Cold glass at night, drafts, and heating vents create microclimates. A plant can experience warm air above and cold roots below.
  • Humidity usually falls: Heating systems dry indoor air, which can stress leaves while the soil stays wet longer.
  • Soil dries more slowly: Less light means less photosynthesis and less water use. Evaporation slows too, so the pot stays damp longer.

These changes explain why a plant that tolerated weekly watering in July may struggle with the same schedule in January. The plant isn’t “being picky”—it’s using less water and energy.

How to Adjust Winter Care: Water, Light, Fertilizer, and Temperature

Watering: Switch from schedule-based to observation-based

In winter, the most common mistake is overwatering. Because roots take up water more slowly, excess moisture can linger, creating low-oxygen conditions that invite rot.

Practical adjustments: - Check moisture deeper than the surface (finger test, chopstick method, or a moisture meter). - Water less often, not necessarily less volume, depending on pot size and soil mix. The goal is thorough watering followed by appropriate drying. - Watch for warning signs: persistent wet soil, sour smell, soft stems, or yellow leaves that drop quickly.

Light: Treat it like a nutrient

Light is the “fuel” that lets plants use water and fertilizer. In winter, you can compensate by: - Moving plants closer to the brightest window (while avoiding cold drafts). - Rotating pots weekly for even exposure. - Using a grow light for light-hungry plants or those that become leggy.

Some foliage plants with slower winter growth still benefit from stable brightness. For example, a vining aroid like philodendron brandtianum silver leaf often maintains better leaf quality through winter when its light is consistent and it isn’t kept in perpetually damp soil.

Fertilizer: Reduce or pause

If growth slows, fertilizer can accumulate in the soil because the plant isn’t using it quickly. That buildup may burn roots or cause leaf tip browning.

General rule: - Pause fertilizing for plants that show little to no new growth. - Use a diluted dose for plants that continue actively growing under strong light (like under grow lights).

Temperature and airflow: Stabilize the root zone

Roots are sensitive to cold. A plant near a window might experience chilled soil even when the room feels warm.

Try: - Keeping pots a few inches away from cold glass. - Avoiding placement directly above heating vents (rapid drying + leaf stress). - Using a saucer that doesn’t trap water against the bottom of the pot.

Species Differences: Dormant, Semi-Dormant, and “Winter Bloomers”

Not all plants interpret winter the same way. Some are triggered more by day length than temperature; others respond primarily to cooler nights.

  • Temperate outdoor perennials: Expect dormancy. Reduce watering once plants die back; protect roots with mulch where appropriate.
  • Tropical houseplants: Often semi-dormant indoors. They may still produce a leaf occasionally, but slowly.
  • Plants with unique seasonal cues: Some species time flowering or growth cycles differently.

A good example of plants with specific seasonal needs is bromeliads. Their watering approach is distinctive year-round (often involving the central “cup”), but winter still changes how quickly moisture evaporates and how long water sits in the crown. If you’re refreshing your routine, reviewing bromeliad care basics can help you align watering and light with their natural growth habits during the darker months.

Winter Pests and Disease: Why Problems Can Increase When Growth Slows

It feels counterintuitive, but pest and disease issues can spike in winter. The cause isn’t winter itself—it’s the indoor conditions and the plant’s reduced ability to “outgrow” damage.

Common winter risk factors: - Overwatered soil leading to root stress and fungus-friendly conditions. - Low humidity encouraging spider mites. - Crowded indoor plant groupings that reduce airflow. - Dusty leaves that lower photosynthesis further, weakening the plant.

Also, when plants are growing slowly, any damage lingers longer. A few mites can become a bigger issue because the plant isn’t rapidly producing healthy new leaves.

If you keep larger statement plants indoors, pay extra attention to leaf undersides, petioles, and the soil line. Alocasias, for instance, are notorious for showing stress quickly when light drops and watering stays too frequent; it’s helpful to know what to look for early by studying guides like common alocasia pests and diseases with photos. Early detection in winter saves months of recovery time later.

Practical Conclusion: A Winter Care Checklist That Respects Dormancy

Winter care differs because your plant’s inputs (light, warmth, humidity) change—and its metabolism follows. The simplest path to success is to stop treating winter like “summer indoors” and instead care for what you see.

Use this winter checklist: - Water only when the root zone actually needs it; expect slower drying. - Increase or stabilize light, especially for plants that get leggy or pale. - Reduce or pause fertilizer unless the plant is actively growing under strong light. - Avoid cold drafts and hot vents; aim for steady temperatures at the pot level. - Monitor pests proactively, because slow growth means slow recovery.

When you treat dormancy as a normal, healthy phase—not a problem to fix—your plants conserve energy through winter and reward you with stronger growth when the brighter months return.
